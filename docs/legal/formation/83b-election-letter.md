## What this document does (plain English)

The 83(b) is the most important tax move most founders will ever make. Here's why:

**Without 83(b):**
- Every time your shares vest (monthly after the cliff), the IRS treats the FMV of the newly-vested shares as ordinary income
- FMV post-seed round might be $1/share (100x your purchase price)
- FMV post-Series A might be $5/share (500x)
- Post-Series B: $20/share (2000x)
- You owe ordinary-income tax on this "phantom income" even though you haven't sold any shares
- Over the vesting period, tax bill can be $500K–$2M+ for a founder on paper, from a company that's illiquid

**With 83(b):**
- You tell the IRS: "Tax me NOW on the current spread, which is zero ($500 FMV − $500 paid = $0)"
- You pay $0 tax today
- All future appreciation is capital gains (at eventual sale), not ordinary income
- You owe nothing until a liquidity event

**Why 30 days and why so strict:**
- The IRS considers 83(b) a strict-deadline election. Courts have upheld this — there is no "substantial compliance" escape hatch.
- The 30-day rule is in the statute itself (26 U.S.C. § 83(b)(2)).
- Certified mail with return receipt provides the proof of mailing date (postmark) that survives any IRS dispute.

**Common founder mistakes:**
- Waiting to mail "until I have more info" — don't. Fill in bracketed fields and mail.
- Sending regular first-class mail — no proof if IRS loses it.
- Sending to the wrong IRS service center — some states have specific centers; use the IRS lookup link.
- Sending the 83(b) to the Corporation instead of the IRS — the CORP copy is separate and NOT a filing.
- Missing the deadline by a day — no remedy.

## Compliance notes

- Statutory basis: 26 U.S.C. § 83(b), Treasury Reg. § 1.83-2.
- Section 83(b)(2): election "not later than 30 days after the date of such transfer."
- IRC § 7503: if 30th day falls on weekend/holiday, deadline extends to next business day.
- Post-Rev. Proc. 2012-29 (updated 2015): no longer need to attach copy to annual tax return, but best practice is to retain with personal records.
- $0 income inclusion is legitimate when FMV = purchase price at transfer. If the corp has already appreciated (e.g., 83(b) filed months after stock issuance — not allowed, but hypothetically), the spread would be ordinary income in the year of transfer.
